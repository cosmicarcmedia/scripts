01 How to Actually Read Your Insurance Policy Anyone
Presenter: Any agent~22 min read

Want changes? Copy the transcript, paste it into an email, edit freely, and send it to landon@cosmicarcmedia.com. Landon updates the script.

Your insurance policy is one of the most important documents you own. It may also be the one you have read the least.

Most people flip through the first page, set it on the counter, and never look at it again. The problem is that the pages people skip are usually the ones that decide whether a claim gets paid.

I'm [Name] with Texas Select Insurance Group in San Angelo. In the next several minutes, I'll show you how to read a policy from front to back in plain English, so it stops feeling like a legal document and starts telling you exactly what you own.

We'll start at the declarations page and work through the parts that almost every policy shares, including limits, deductibles, exclusions, and the endorsements that quietly change your coverage.

THE DECLARATIONS PAGE: YOUR SUMMARY SHEET

When your policy arrives, the first thing you'll see is the declarations page. Your agent or company will sometimes call it the "dec page" for short. Think of it as the cover sheet for the whole policy. It's the one page, sometimes two, that gives you a snapshot of everything.

The declarations page tells you who is covered. The named insured is the person or persons specifically listed on the policy. In most cases that's you, and if you're married, your spouse. It also tells you the policy period, which is the start and end date of your coverage. If something happens before that start date or after that end date, your policy generally won't respond.

It lists what is covered at a high level. For a home policy, that might be dwelling coverage for the structure, personal property coverage for your belongings, and liability coverage. For an auto policy, you'll see things like liability limits, comprehensive, and collision. Each of those coverages has a limit shown right there on the declarations page, and that limit is the maximum the policy will pay for that type of loss.

You'll also see your deductible, which we'll talk about in more detail in a minute, and you'll see your premium, which is the amount you're paying for the policy.

The declarations page is the most practical page in the document. If you only read one page, read that one. But it's the pages after it where the real detail lives. And those pages are what we're working through right now.

THE SIX MAIN SECTIONS OF A POLICY

Almost every insurance policy, whether it's for your home, your car, or a commercial property, is organized into the same basic sections. They're not always labeled identically, but the structure is consistent. Once you know what to look for, you can find your way around any policy.

The first section is the declarations page, which we just covered. That's your summary.

The second section is called the insuring agreement. This is where the insurance company makes its promise to you. It's usually pretty short. It says something along the lines of: we will pay for covered losses subject to the terms and conditions of this policy. The insuring agreement describes in broad terms what coverage you're getting. It's important because it establishes the foundation of the contract.

The third section is definitions. This is one of the most overlooked parts of a policy, and it's actually really valuable. Insurance policies use specific terms that have specific meanings within the document, and the definitions section explains what those terms mean. Words like "occurrence," "property damage," "covered loss," "insured location," all of those have defined meanings that affect how your coverage applies. When you're reading your policy and you come across a word in quotation marks, that's a signal. The policy is using a defined term. Go back to the definitions section and look it up. The meaning might be narrower than you'd expect, and that matters.

The fourth section is the exclusions. This is where the policy lists what it will not cover. Exclusions often get a bad reputation, but they exist for a reason. Insurance is priced to cover certain risks and not others. The exclusions section is where the company draws that line. We'll talk more about how to actually read the exclusions in a few minutes, because there's a practical way to approach them.

The fifth section is conditions. This is where you'll find the rules that both you and the insurance company have to follow for the policy to work. Things like: you must notify the company promptly after a loss. You must cooperate with the investigation. You must protect the property from further damage. Conditions also explain things like how disputes are handled and what happens if there are multiple policies that cover the same loss. These aren't fine print traps. They're reasonable obligations on both sides.

The sixth section is endorsements. Endorsements are attachments to your policy that change the base coverage. They can add coverage, remove coverage, or modify how a coverage works. Some endorsements come standard with your policy. Others are optional add-ons you or your agent selected. We'll talk more about endorsements specifically a bit later.

Reading these sections in order actually makes sense once you understand how they relate to each other. The insuring agreement says yes in broad terms. The exclusions say no to specific things. The conditions say here's how this all works. The endorsements say here's what we've adjusted.

COVERAGE LIMITS: WHAT THE NUMBERS ACTUALLY MEAN

Let's talk about coverage limits, because this comes up constantly in conversations about insurance and the terms can be confusing.

A coverage limit is the maximum dollar amount your policy will pay for a specific type of loss. If your homeowners policy has a dwelling coverage limit of two hundred thousand dollars, that's the most the company will pay to repair or rebuild your home under that coverage. If the actual cost turns out to be higher, the difference would be on you.

Two terms you'll see frequently, especially in liability coverage and commercial policies, are per-occurrence limit and aggregate limit. A per-occurrence limit is the maximum paid for any single event or incident. An aggregate limit is the maximum the policy will pay across all claims during the entire policy period. Think of the aggregate as a bucket. Every claim you file dips into that bucket. Once the bucket is empty, the policy won't pay more claims for that period, even if individual losses don't exceed the per-occurrence limit.

On a personal auto or home policy, you'll usually see per-occurrence language for liability. On commercial policies, you'll see both per-occurrence and aggregate limits used together. If you're a business owner and you carry a commercial general liability policy, that distinction matters quite a bit.

One thing worth checking on your home policy is whether your dwelling coverage limit is actually enough to rebuild your home at today's construction costs. Construction costs in West Texas, like everywhere else, have moved significantly over the past several years. Labor and materials are more expensive than they used to be. If your policy was set a while ago and you haven't revisited the coverage amount, it's worth having a conversation with your agent about whether your current limit still reflects what it would actually cost to rebuild. Most companies recommend insuring your home for its full replacement cost. That's different from the market value or the assessed value. It's what it would actually cost to build it again from the ground up.

DEDUCTIBLES: HOW THEY ACTUALLY WORK

The deductible is the amount you pay out of pocket before your insurance kicks in on a claim. Here's the most straightforward way to think about it. If you have a claim worth three thousand dollars and your deductible is one thousand dollars, the insurance company pays two thousand and you pay one thousand.

There are a few things people sometimes get surprised by that are worth knowing in advance.

First, deductibles apply per claim, not per year. This is different from how health insurance often works, where you have one deductible for the whole year. With home and auto insurance, in most cases, each claim event has its own deductible. So if you have two separate claims in the same year, you'd pay the deductible twice.

Second, some policies have different deductibles for different types of losses. On a homeowners policy in Texas, it's common to have a separate, higher deductible specifically for wind and hail damage. That deductible is often expressed as a percentage of your dwelling coverage rather than a flat dollar amount. So if your home is insured for two hundred thousand dollars and you have a two percent wind and hail deductible, that's a four thousand dollar deductible on those specific claims. That's important to know before a hailstorm comes through San Angelo. You don't want to be surprised by that number when you're already dealing with damage.

Third, your deductible is not something the insurance company can waive, and a contractor cannot legally waive it for you in Texas. If a contractor offers to eat your deductible, that's a red flag and it's actually a violation of Texas law. The deductible is your share of the claim. It exists because it creates a shared financial stake in the outcome and it prevents very small claims from being filed constantly.

Choosing a higher deductible typically lowers your premium. Choosing a lower deductible means more predictable out-of-pocket costs if something happens. There's no universal right answer. It depends on your financial situation. Talk to your agent about what makes sense for you.

HOW TO FIND AND READ THE EXCLUSIONS

The exclusions section is the one most people skip, and it's the one that comes up most often in claims disputes. So let's talk about how to actually approach it.

First, find it. In most home and auto policies, there's a section specifically labeled Exclusions. It might also appear within each coverage section. Some policies have a general exclusions section plus specific exclusions within individual coverages. Read both.

The exclusions are not tricks. They're the lines the policy draws around what it's designed to cover. Standard home insurance in Texas, for example, does not cover flood damage. That's not buried in fine print. It's a clearly listed exclusion. Flood damage requires separate flood insurance, typically through the National Flood Insurance Program or through a private carrier. Similarly, earthquake damage is excluded from most standard home policies.

When you read an exclusion, pay attention to the language. Some exclusions are absolute. Others have exceptions. You'll sometimes see language like "this policy does not cover damage caused by... except as provided by endorsement..." That "except" matters. It means there might be a way to buy back the coverage through an add-on.

For example, a standard home policy in Texas typically excludes backup of sewers and drains. But many companies offer an endorsement that you can add, for a separate premium, that provides coverage for exactly that situation. If you've ever had a bathtub back up or a drain overflow in your home, you know that can cause real damage. Knowing the exclusion exists means you can have an informed conversation with your agent about whether the endorsement makes sense for you.

A practical way to use the exclusions section: when you're reviewing your policy, ask yourself what the most likely causes of loss are for your specific property. In the Concho Valley, wind and hail are real risks. A pipe freezing during a cold snap is a real risk. Flooding after a heavy rain event is a real risk. Check each of those against the exclusions section. If something you're worried about is excluded, you have options. You can ask about endorsements, you can look at separate policies, or you can at minimum understand where your exposure is.

ENDORSEMENTS AND RIDERS: HOW COVERAGE GETS CUSTOMIZED

An endorsement, sometimes called a rider, is a document that attaches to your policy and changes it in some way. Endorsements can add coverage that wasn't there, remove coverage, change a limit, or modify how a term is defined. They are a normal, everyday part of how insurance works.

Some endorsements are attached automatically, meaning the company includes them as part of the standard policy in your state. Others are optional, meaning you chose to add them, or your agent recommended them, at the time of purchase.

When you receive your policy, the endorsements will typically be listed on the declarations page or attached at the back of the policy document. Each endorsement has its own form number and a description. It's worth reading each one, because endorsements can significantly change what your policy does.

Let me give you a couple of real-world examples. Say you own an older home and you've done some renovation work. There's an endorsement called an ordinance or law endorsement, or sometimes building code coverage, that pays for the additional costs required to bring a structure up to current building codes when you repair or rebuild after a covered loss. Without that endorsement, if your city requires updated electrical or plumbing standards during reconstruction, those upgraded costs might not be covered. With the endorsement, they often are, depending on the policy. That's worth knowing about before you need it.

Another common endorsement covers scheduled personal property, which means you list specific high-value items, like jewelry, musical instruments, or collectibles, and insure them individually at an agreed value. Standard home policies have sub-limits on jewelry and similar items. If the standard limit isn't enough for what you own, a scheduled endorsement is the tool for that.

If you're not sure what endorsements are on your current policy, call your agent and ask them to walk you through it. That's exactly the kind of conversation we're here for.

REPLACEMENT COST VS. ACTUAL CASH VALUE

This is one of the most important distinctions in all of property insurance, and it comes up in almost every claims conversation.

Replacement cost coverage pays to repair or replace your damaged property at current prices. So if your roof is damaged and it costs ten thousand dollars to replace it today, a replacement cost policy is going to base the payment on that ten thousand dollar figure, minus your deductible.

Actual cash value coverage pays replacement cost minus depreciation. Depreciation is the reduction in value due to age and wear. So that same ten thousand dollar roof, if it's ten years old, might have an actual cash value of seven thousand dollars. That's what an actual cash value policy would pay, minus your deductible. You'd be responsible for making up the rest of the gap.

The Texas Department of Insurance lays this out clearly on their website with a straightforward example, and the numbers can be pretty significant. On a twenty-year-old roof, the difference between what a replacement cost policy pays and what an actual cash value policy pays could be the difference between your claim covering the repairs or you paying a large portion out of pocket.

Here's something else worth knowing about how replacement cost policies actually work in practice. With many policies, when you have a covered loss, the insurance company doesn't necessarily write you one check for the full replacement cost right away. In many cases they'll pay actual cash value first, which is the depreciated amount. Then, once you've completed the repairs and submitted proof, they release the remaining held-back amount, sometimes called recoverable depreciation. So the total payment ends up being the full replacement cost, but it comes in two stages. We'll talk more about that process in the claims video. For now, just know that it's worth understanding which type of coverage you have and how payment works under your specific policy.

Actual cash value policies do carry lower premiums, so there's a trade-off. The right choice depends on your situation. Talk to your agent about your specific policy and what it would mean for your most likely claim scenarios.

WHAT TO CHECK AT EVERY RENEWAL

Your policy renews every year. Most people just pay the bill and move on, and I understand why. But taking ten minutes at renewal to review a few things can make a real difference.

Start with the declarations page. Has anything changed from last year? Look at your coverage limits, your deductibles, and your premium. If your premium went up, do you know why? Sometimes it's a market adjustment. Sometimes it's a claims surcharge. Sometimes there's an error worth catching.

Think about whether anything has changed about your property or your life in the past year. Did you renovate your kitchen or add a room? Did you buy expensive equipment, jewelry, or electronics? Did you start a home-based business? All of those things can affect whether your current coverage is still the right fit. Standard home policies often have limited or no coverage for business equipment used for commercial purposes in your home. If that applies to you, it's worth a conversation.

Look at your dwelling coverage limit and think about whether it still reflects current construction costs. As I mentioned earlier, costs have moved. If you're insuring a home you bought or last reviewed several years ago, it may be worth asking your agent to run a replacement cost estimate.

Check your endorsements. Are they still the right ones for your situation? Has something you used to need gone away, or has a new risk come into play?

And finally, if you've had a claim in the past year, look at your renewal carefully. Your rate may have adjusted. In Texas, companies can factor claims history into your premium, and they can also nonrenew policies under certain circumstances. Knowing the rules around that, and what your options are, is something your agent can help you understand.

The broader point is this: your insurance policy is a living document in the sense that your needs change over time. Reading it at renewal, even just the dec page and any endorsement changes, keeps you from getting caught off guard.

RECAP AND CLOSE

So let's bring this together quickly.

Your declarations page is the summary. Named insured, policy period, coverages, limits, deductibles, premium. Start there every time.

The six sections of a policy are: the declarations, the insuring agreement, the definitions, the exclusions, the conditions, and endorsements. Each one has a job, and understanding what that job is helps you navigate the document.

Coverage limits are the maximum the policy will pay. Per-occurrence limits apply to a single event. Aggregate limits cap total payouts over the policy period.

Deductibles are your share of each claim. In Texas, watch for separate percentage-based deductibles on wind and hail.

The exclusions section tells you what's not covered. Read it alongside the endorsements, because sometimes what's excluded in the base policy can be added back.

Endorsements customize your coverage. Know what's on your policy.

Replacement cost pays current prices. Actual cash value subtracts depreciation. Which one you have matters a lot when you have a claim.

And at every renewal, take ten minutes to review the dec page, check your limits, and make sure your policy still reflects your situation.

If you have questions about your current policy, or if you'd like us to walk through it with you, give us a call. We're happy to do a policy review at any time, no obligation. That's what we're here for.

02 Claims, Start to Finish: What Really Happens After You Call Anyone
Presenter: Any agent~26 min read

Most people only file an insurance claim a handful of times in their entire life. Which means almost no one actually knows how the process works until they are standing in the middle of it, usually on a bad day.

I'm [Name] with Texas Select Insurance Group in San Angelo, and I want to take the mystery out of it. I'll walk you through exactly what happens after you report a loss, from the first phone call to the final payment.

We'll follow a home claim and an auto claim from start to finish, explain the terms you'll run into along the way, and answer the question almost everyone asks: does filing a claim raise your rate?

THE FIRST HOURS: DOCUMENT EVERYTHING

Something goes wrong. A hailstorm comes through and damages your roof. A pipe bursts while you're away from home. Someone runs a red light and hits your vehicle. The first few hours after a loss are more important than most people realize.

Before you do anything else, take photos and video. Document the damage as completely as you can before any cleanup or repairs happen. Walk around. Get close-up shots and wide shots. Capture the context, not just the damage itself. If you're dealing with a home loss, photograph every room that's affected. If it's your vehicle, photograph it from every angle, including detail shots of the points of impact.

The reason this matters is straightforward. Your claim is going to be evaluated based on documented evidence of the loss. The more thorough your documentation is, the clearer the picture for everyone involved. If you clean up or make repairs before documenting, important information about the original damage can be lost.

That said, if your property is exposed to further damage, you should take reasonable steps to prevent it. If a window is broken, board it up. If part of your roof is damaged and rain is in the forecast, tarping it is appropriate. Your policy actually requires you to protect the property from further damage where you reasonably can. Keep receipts for any materials you buy to make those temporary repairs. In most cases those costs are reimbursable.

One more thing for the first hours: if you're dealing with a home loss, make sure the property is safe before you go back in. And for an auto accident, document the scene before vehicles are moved if it's safe to do so. Get the other driver's information, insurance information, and if there are witnesses, get their contact information as well.

Think of this first phase as building your file. The claim doesn't start when you call the insurance company. It starts when the loss happens. And the records you make in those first hours become the foundation of your claim.

FIRST NOTICE OF LOSS: MAKING THE CALL

First notice of loss, or FNOL, is the formal term for when you first report the claim to your insurance company. For most people, that means calling the claims line or, for many companies these days, submitting through an app or website.

You should notify your company as soon as reasonably possible after a loss. Texas home policies generally have a one-year deadline to file a claim from the date of damage, but the practical advice is not to wait. The sooner you report, the sooner the process moves. And policies often include language requiring prompt notice.

When you make that first call, have your policy number ready, along with the date and description of the loss, the location of the loss, and the contact information where you can be reached. The representative taking the call will open your claim and assign it a claim number. Write that number down. You'll use it for every subsequent call and piece of correspondence.

At this stage, you don't need to have everything figured out. You're opening the file. You'll have time to provide additional documentation and details as the process moves forward.

If your home was damaged and you have a mortgage, your mortgage company has an interest in the claim as well. We'll talk about how that affects the payment process in a few minutes. But for now, just know that your lender will likely be involved in the settlement.

For our example, let's follow two claims. The first is Maria, who lives in San Angelo. A spring hailstorm comes through and damages her roof and a few windows. The second is David, who was driving on Sherwood Way when another driver ran a red light and hit his truck. Both of them call their insurance companies the same day. Both get claim numbers and are told to expect contact from an adjuster. That's the next step.

THE ADJUSTER'S ROLE: WHO'S WHO

Once your claim is opened, it gets assigned to a claims adjuster. The adjuster's job is to investigate the loss, evaluate the damage, and determine what the policy covers and what the payment should be.

The adjuster working your claim works for the insurance company. They are not working against you, but they do represent the company's interest in making sure the claim is evaluated accurately. They're looking at what the policy says, what the damage is, and what it costs to repair or replace it. In many cases, insurance company adjusters are thorough and fair. Their estimate is the starting point for the settlement.

For larger or more complex claims, some people choose to hire a public adjuster. A public adjuster is a licensed professional who works for you, not the insurance company. They help evaluate the damage, prepare the claim, and negotiate with the company on your behalf. They charge a fee for this service, typically a percentage of the claim settlement, so you'll want to understand the cost before hiring one. In Texas, public adjusters must be licensed by the Texas Department of Insurance. If you're considering hiring one, you can verify their license through TDI's website or by calling their Help Line at 800-252-3439.

You don't need a public adjuster for most straightforward claims. But if your loss is large, complex, or if you're uncertain about the process, it's a legitimate option to know about.

For Maria's hail claim, an insurance adjuster comes out to inspect the roof and windows. For David's auto claim, the company directs him to take the truck to a preferred repair shop or arranges for an adjuster to inspect it. In both cases, the adjuster's estimate is what the company will use as the basis for their offer.

PROOF OF LOSS AND THE CLAIM PROCESS

After your claim is reported and the initial investigation begins, your insurance company may ask you to submit a proof of loss form. A proof of loss is a formal, signed statement that documents the details of your claim. It lists what was damaged or lost, the estimated value, and confirms that the information is accurate to the best of your knowledge. Some policies require it to be notarized.

Not every claim requires a separate proof of loss form. For a simple auto claim, the photos and repair estimate may be sufficient documentation. For a larger home claim, especially one involving extensive personal property losses, a proof of loss with a detailed inventory is often part of the process.

If your company asks for a proof of loss, provide it as completely and accurately as you can. Don't estimate things loosely. If your belongings were damaged or destroyed, make a detailed list. Include descriptions, approximate ages, and values. If you have receipts or photos of your items from before the loss, those help. This is actually one of the best arguments for keeping a home inventory. It's a list and photos of your belongings, updated periodically. TDI has a home inventory checklist on their website as a free resource. It's a straightforward way to be prepared.

For Maria's claim, her insurance company sends her a form to document her personal property damage inside the home, including windows and some items in a room where the ceiling leaked. For David's auto claim, the proof process is simpler. The shop submits a written repair estimate, and David documents any personal property that was in the vehicle and was damaged.

One thing the TDI guidance is clear on: keep copies of every document you submit, and keep a log of every phone call you have with the insurance company. Write down the date, who you spoke with, and what was discussed. If there are disagreements later about what was communicated, those notes matter.

HOW THE DEDUCTIBLE APPLIES

When the adjuster's estimate is complete and the company accepts the claim, the deductible comes off the top of the payment. The deductible is your share of the loss, so the company pays the covered loss amount minus your deductible.

For Maria's hail damage, let's say the adjuster estimates the roof replacement and window repairs at twelve thousand dollars. She has a two percent deductible on wind and hail, and her home is insured for two hundred thousand dollars. That means her wind and hail deductible is four thousand dollars. Her insurance company's share of the repair cost, before factoring in any depreciation, is eight thousand dollars.

For David's auto claim, his deductible on collision coverage is a thousand dollars. The repair estimate is six thousand dollars. The company will pay five thousand, and David pays his thousand dollar deductible to the shop when the work is completed.

The deductible is straightforward in concept, but people sometimes expect to receive a check for the full estimated cost of repairs before their deductible is accounted for. That's not how it works. The deductible comes off first, and you're responsible for that amount directly.

REPLACEMENT COST, ACTUAL CASH VALUE, AND RECOVERABLE DEPRECIATION

Here's where replacement cost and actual cash value actually play out in a real claim, and this is something worth understanding in advance.

If your policy has replacement cost coverage, in many cases the company doesn't pay the full replacement cost all at once. What typically happens is they pay actual cash value first. Actual cash value is the replacement cost minus depreciation. Depreciation accounts for the age and condition of the damaged property.

So for Maria's roof claim, if the full replacement cost is twelve thousand dollars and the roof is ten years old, the insurance company might calculate the actual cash value at, say, eight thousand dollars. After her four thousand dollar wind and hail deductible, the initial payment would be four thousand dollars. That held-back difference between replacement cost and actual cash value is called recoverable depreciation. Once Maria completes the roof replacement and submits the contractor's invoice showing the work was done, the company releases that held-back amount, bringing the total payment up to the full replacement cost minus the deductible.

So with a replacement cost policy, you often get two payments. The first covers the actual cash value of the loss, minus your deductible. The second comes after you've made the repairs and provided proof, and it covers the depreciation that was initially held back.

There are usually time limits on how long you have to complete repairs and submit for the recoverable depreciation. Ask your adjuster or check your policy for that window.

If your policy is actual cash value only, there is no second payment. The depreciated amount is the final settlement. This is a key reason why replacement cost coverage is generally recommended for your home and often for your vehicle as well, depending on its age and value.

One more thing to know here. For Maria, because she has a mortgage on her home, the check for structural repairs will be made out to both her and her mortgage company. She'll need to endorse it and send it to the lender, who will then release the funds as the repair work progresses. The TDI guidance is that once the mortgage company has the information they need, they must release money to you within ten days. If there's a dispute with your lender over those funds, the Texas Attorney General's office handles those complaints.

THE RESERVATION OF RIGHTS LETTER

If you receive a letter from your insurance company during the claims process that says "reservation of rights" at the top, don't panic. But do read it carefully.

A reservation of rights letter is the company saying: we are investigating this claim and we're going to continue processing it, but we want to let you know that we may have some questions about whether and how the policy covers this loss. By handling the claim and paying certain costs, we are not giving up our right to later determine that some or all of the loss isn't covered.

It's essentially a way for the company to continue working on your claim without waiving any coverage defenses they might have under the policy. It doesn't mean your claim is denied. It means there's a coverage question in play that they're still evaluating.

If you receive one of these, it's worth calling your agent and asking them to explain it in the context of your specific situation. In some cases it's routine. In others, it signals a more complex coverage question where you may want to pay closer attention to the process.

You might see a reservation of rights letter if there's a question about whether the cause of the loss is covered, if the investigation is ongoing and the circumstances aren't fully established yet, or if there are potentially multiple coverage issues at play. The letter is the company being transparent that those questions exist, rather than just processing the claim as if everything is clearly covered.

TEXAS CLAIM HANDLING TIMELINES

Texas has specific rules about how quickly insurance companies have to act on your claim. These are governed by the Texas prompt payment of claims statutes, and the Texas Department of Insurance enforces them.

Here is what the law requires. After you file your claim, your insurance company has fifteen days to acknowledge that they received it. Within that same fifteen day window, they must provide you with any forms you need to file and let you know what information they need from you.

Once you've submitted everything the company needs, they have fifteen business days to accept or deny your claim. If they need additional time, they can extend that by up to forty-five days, but they must notify you in writing of the reason for the delay.

After the company agrees to pay a claim, they must send you the check or direct payment within five business days.

If the company misses that five business day payment deadline after agreeing to pay, you may have the right to sue for the claim amount plus interest and attorney's fees under Texas law.

There are some exceptions worth knowing. After a major declared disaster, the company may have additional time. If the company suspects arson, they have thirty days to accept or deny the claim. For the Texas Windstorm Insurance Association specifically, they have sixty days to accept or deny, and ten days to pay after accepting.

For Maria's hail claim, she should expect contact from an adjuster promptly after filing. This requirement exists because insurance is priced based on the assumption that you're carrying adequate coverage. If everyone underinsured their buildings by 50 percent, the math that underlies the entire system breaks down.

So the policy includes a clause that says: if you don't carry at least the required percentage of your building's replacement cost as your coverage limit, and a loss occurs, your payout will be reduced in proportion to how far short you fell.

Let me walk through a specific number so this is concrete.

Say you own a commercial building in San Angelo. Maybe it's a shop, a small warehouse, an office building. Let's say the true replacement cost of that building today is $1,000,000. Your policy has an 80 percent coinsurance requirement, which means you're supposed to carry at least $800,000 in coverage.

Now suppose when you set up the policy years ago, you put the coverage limit at $600,000. You thought that was a reasonable number. The building is paid off, you've had it for a while, that's what felt right. Nobody told you there was a formula that needed to be met.

A storm comes through. You have a covered loss. Say the damage is $200,000, which is a serious but partial loss, not a total loss.

Here's what the coinsurance clause does. The insurance company looks at how much coverage you actually carried, $600,000, and divides it by how much you were supposed to carry, $800,000. That gives you 0.75, or 75 percent. That ratio is applied to your claim.

So instead of receiving the full $200,000 repair cost, minus your deductible, you receive 75 percent of it. That's $150,000 before your deductible. The remaining $50,000 is on you, because the coinsurance clause treats the difference as if you self-insured that portion. You paid premiums, you had a covered loss, and you still have a $50,000 gap because your building was underinsured at the time of the claim.

That outcome surprises a lot of people, and understandably so. The key thing to understand is that this is not a penalty in the punitive sense. It's how the math works when the coverage limit doesn't match the actual value. The system assumes you agreed to insure to value, and if you didn't, the claim settlement reflects the ratio of what you carried to what you should have carried.

The fix is straightforward in concept: make sure your coverage limit accurately reflects what it would actually cost to rebuild your building today.

SEGMENT 3: REPLACEMENT COST VS. ACTUAL CASH VALUE, AND HOW DEPRECIATION ENTERS THE PICTURE

Now let's talk about how the loss is actually calculated once a claim is triggered, because this is where replacement cost versus actual cash value becomes important.

Replacement cost coverage, which we've been talking about, means the insurance company pays what it actually costs to repair or rebuild the property at current prices, without deducting for the fact that your building was 20 years old or your roof had some age on it.

Actual cash value, often abbreviated ACV, is replacement cost minus depreciation. The insurance company takes into account the age and condition of the damaged property and reduces the payout accordingly. A 15-year-old roof gets depreciated. A building with older mechanical systems gets depreciated. You receive what the property was worth in its current condition, not what it costs to put it back new.

Actual cash value coverage is less expensive. If budget is a real constraint, it's a legitimate option for some operations. But the gap between what ACV pays and what it actually costs to rebuild can be significant, especially on older structures, and you should go into that choice with clear eyes about what it means at claim time.

Most commercial property policies for owner-occupied buildings are written on a replacement cost basis, which is generally the right approach. The critical thing is to make sure the limit of insurance actually reflects current replacement cost, because even a replacement cost policy can leave you with a shortfall if the limit is set too low and the coinsurance clause triggers.

SEGMENT 4: AGREED VALUE, AND HOW IT HANDLES THE COINSURANCE PROBLEM

There's an alternative to navigating the coinsurance formula at every renewal, and it's called agreed value coverage.

Agreed value is an endorsement that suspends the coinsurance clause. Instead of the insurance company applying a coinsurance formula at claim time, you and the insurer agree ahead of time on the insured value of the building, and that value is what governs the claim. If a total loss occurs, the policy pays the agreed value. If a partial loss occurs, it pays the repair cost without a coinsurance penalty.

The requirement for agreed value is that you submit a statement of values to your insurer before the policy goes into effect. A statement of values is a formal document that describes your insured property and assigns a value to it. The insurer reviews it, and if they accept it, they agree that the stated value is adequate. From that point, the coinsurance clause is suspended for the policy period.

This is a cleaner approach for many commercial property owners because it removes the coinsurance calculation from the equation. However, the agreed value endorsement has an expiration date. It's typically tied to the policy term. When the policy renews, if you don't resubmit a statement of values and get the endorsement renewed, the coinsurance clause quietly comes back. This is one of those renewal conversations worth having every year.

SEGMENT 5: INFLATION GUARD AND KEEPING YOUR COVERAGE CURRENT

Even if your building was correctly valued and correctly insured when the policy was set up, construction costs don't stand still. Labor costs, material prices, and building supply chains all move over time, sometimes significantly.

An inflation guard endorsement is an automatic mechanism that increases your coverage limit by a set percentage each year to help keep pace with rising construction costs. Typical inflation guard rates are in the range of 2 to 4 percent annually. The endorsement doesn't require you to do anything year to year, it simply increases the limit on a predetermined schedule.

The honest caveat is that inflation guard rates don't always match actual construction cost inflation. In periods when construction costs rise sharply, a 2 or 3 percent annual adjustment may not be sufficient to close the gap. The endorsement is a useful tool, but it's not a substitute for periodically doing a real replacement cost appraisal or having a genuine conversation with your agent about whether your current limit still reflects what it would cost to rebuild your building.

A good rule of thumb is to revisit your commercial property limits whenever you make significant improvements to the building, whenever you hear from contractors that costs in your area have changed significantly, and at minimum every few years even if nothing has changed on the surface.

Ordinance or law coverage. This one comes up after a loss, and it surprises people when it isn't there.

That can mean a new electrical system, updated plumbing, additional fire suppression, accessibility requirements under current ADA standards, and other mandated improvements.

Standard commercial property coverage pays to rebuild what was there. It doesn't automatically pay for the additional cost of meeting current code requirements. Ordinance or law coverage fills that gap.

There are typically three components to ordinance or law coverage. The first covers the loss in value of the undamaged portion of the building when the whole structure has to come down because local code doesn't allow you to repair a building that's more than a certain percentage damaged. The second covers the demolition cost of removing that undamaged portion. The third covers the increased cost of construction to meet current code when rebuilding.

For an older commercial building, particularly here in West Texas where there are a lot of structures that have been in use for many years, ordinance or law coverage is worth a specific conversation. Business interruption. This connects directly to your commercial property policy. When a covered event damages your building and forces you to stop operating, or reduces your ability to operate, business interruption coverage, also called business income coverage, is designed to replace the revenue you would have earned during the period your business is shut down or impaired.

What it covers varies by policy, but it commonly includes lost net income, ongoing payroll, rent or mortgage payments, and certain other fixed operating expenses you'd still owe even if the doors were closed.

Business interruption is triggered by a covered property loss. It doesn't work without an underlying property claim. The limit and the period of coverage both matter. For businesses with complex operations or high fixed expenses, it's worth thinking through realistically how long it would take to rebuild and be fully operational after a major loss, and making sure the business interruption limit and duration reflect that scenario.

Workers' compensation, professional liability, commercial auto, and cyber liability are typically not included and may need to be added or purchased separately depending on your situation.

Some carriers let you customize a BOP with endorsements, which are basically add-ons that expand coverage for specific things like data breach, employment practices, or equipment breakdown.

If you're starting a business or reviewing your coverage, a BOP is a logical place to begin the conversation with your agent. It gives you a solid floor without the complexity of assembling five separate policies from scratch.

QWhat happens if a customer slips and falls in my store?

This is exactly the kind of scenario general liability is built for. Let's walk through how it actually works.

Someone comes into your retail location, slips on a wet floor near the entrance after a rainstorm, and injures their knee. They go to the emergency room. They call you the next week and say they want their medical bills paid.

If you have general liability coverage, you would notify your insurance carrier. The carrier would investigate the claim, review the circumstances, and handle communication with the injured party. If the claim is valid and within your policy limits, the insurance company pays the medical bills. If the person decides to sue, your carrier also provides legal defense, which is significant because attorney fees alone can be expensive even in cases where you're ultimately not found liable.

Now, a few important notes. Coverage depends on your specific policy. Your policy will have limits, meaning there's a maximum amount it will pay per occurrence and per year. If a claim exceeds those limits, you'd be responsible for the difference. This is why choosing appropriate coverage limits matters, and your agent can help you think through what's reasonable for your type of business and foot traffic.

Also, general liability typically covers bodily injury to guests and customers. It does not cover injuries to your own employees. That's what workers' compensation is designed for.

And of course, no one can promise that a specific claim will be covered without looking at the details of the policy. But having general liability in place means you have a response system when something goes wrong, instead of trying to figure it out on the fly.

QDo I need separate insurance for a business I run from home?

This is a question we hear more and more, especially as people run consulting practices, photography businesses, online retail operations, and other ventures out of their homes.

Here's what most people don't realize: your homeowner's insurance policy almost certainly does not cover your business activities. Standard homeowner's policies include very limited, if any, coverage for business property, and they typically exclude liability claims arising from business operations conducted at home.

What does that mean practically? Say you're a photographer who stores several thousand dollars of camera equipment at home. Your homeowner's policy may have a cap of a few hundred dollars on business equipment. If your gear is stolen or damaged in a fire, you could be significantly underinsured.

Or say a client comes to your home office to sign a contract, slips on your front steps, and gets hurt. Your homeowner's policy may deny the liability claim because the visit was for business purposes.

The solutions depend on how substantial your operation is. For lower-risk, lower-revenue home businesses, some carriers offer a home business endorsement you can add to your homeowner's policy to extend coverage for business property and limited liability. For more substantial operations, a separate commercial policy or BOP may make more sense.

The key thing is to have an honest conversation with your agent about what your home-based business actually involves, and not assume your existing homeowner's coverage has it handled. In most cases, it doesn't.

QWhat is a certificate of insurance, and why do clients ask for one?

If you've ever been asked to "send your COI" before starting a job, this is what they're talking about.

A certificate of insurance is a document your insurance carrier provides that summarizes your current coverage. It typically shows what type of policies you have, the carrier's name, your policy numbers, the coverage limits, and the effective and expiration dates.

Clients, landlords, and general contractors ask for certificates of insurance for a straightforward reason: they want to verify that you actually have coverage before you step onto their property, work in their building, or connect with their operations in any way. It's a way of confirming that if something goes wrong, there's insurance in place to respond.

In many commercial contracts, maintaining certain types and levels of insurance is a contractual requirement. If you don't meet it, you can be in breach of contract before you've even started work.

A COI is not the same as your policy, and it's not a guarantee of coverage. It's a snapshot of what's currently in force. Your agent or carrier can issue one, usually pretty quickly, whenever a client requests it.

One thing to be aware of: some clients will ask to be listed as an "additional insured" on your policy. That's a slightly different thing, and it means they get some of the protections of your policy for work you're doing on their behalf. Your agent can walk you through whether that makes sense and how to set it up.

If you're a business owner who does contract work, having your COI easy to access and keeping your policies current is just good business practice.

QWhat is professional liability insurance, or errors and omissions?

Professional liability insurance, often called E&O or errors and omissions coverage, protects you if a client claims that the professional service you provided caused them financial harm.

General liability covers physical injuries and property damage. Professional liability covers the work itself.

Here's an example. You're a bookkeeper. A client claims that an error in their financial records caused them to underpay their taxes, resulting in penalties and back payments. They sue you for the cost. Professional liability would help cover your legal defense and any settlement or judgment up to your policy limits.

Or say you're an IT consultant. You install a software system for a client and there's a flaw in the implementation that causes them to lose data. The client holds you responsible for the cost of recovery. Again, professional liability is what responds.

Any business that provides professional advice, designs, analysis, or services to clients should seriously consider this coverage. That includes consultants, designers, accountants, engineers, real estate agents, insurance agents, healthcare workers, and many others.

General liability and a BOP do not typically include professional liability. It's usually a separate policy or endorsement. The cost varies based on your industry, the size of your contracts, and your claims history.

The thing to understand is that even a meritless claim requires a legal defense, and that gets expensive fast. Professional liability is what keeps that from becoming a personal financial catastrophe.

QDo I need commercial auto insurance if I use my personal truck for work?

The short answer is: if you're regularly using your vehicle for business purposes, your personal auto policy may not cover losses that happen during those activities.

Personal auto policies are designed to cover personal driving. When you're using your vehicle to carry tools, make deliveries, transport equipment, or visit job sites, that use often falls outside what personal auto covers. Some policies have explicit exclusions for business use. Others cover incidental business driving but not regular commercial use.

The concern isn't just getting caught without coverage when something happens. It's the specific situation of having an accident while doing something business-related and having your personal insurer decline the claim because you were in the course of business activities at the time.

Commercial auto insurance is designed to cover that gap. It works similarly to personal auto in structure, with liability, collision, and comprehensive options, but it's underwritten with business use in mind. It can also typically cover higher limits because commercial situations often involve more exposure.

If you have employees who drive vehicles for work, even their personal vehicles, that's another layer of complexity to discuss with your agent. There's a type of coverage called hired and non-owned auto liability that covers situations where employees are driving personal or rented vehicles on company business.

The practical question to ask yourself is: if I get into an accident on the way to a job site with a truck bed full of tools and equipment, would my personal policy respond? If you're not sure, that's worth a conversation with your agent.

QDo I need cyber insurance for a small business?

Cyber insurance is one of those coverages that people in small towns tend to think is for big companies. It's not.

Cyber liability insurance covers losses related to data breaches, ransomware attacks, and other cyber incidents. If your business stores customer information, takes payments digitally, uses email to send sensitive documents, or relies on any kind of software or network, you have some level of cyber exposure.

Consider what a breach actually involves. You may be legally required to notify affected customers. You may need to hire IT specialists to assess and contain the damage. If customer data was compromised, there could be regulatory fines depending on your industry. And if your systems are locked by ransomware and you can't operate, there's lost revenue while you get back up.

Cyber coverage typically addresses several things: costs of notifying customers, forensic investigation, regulatory defense, data recovery, and sometimes business interruption losses from system downtime.

Small businesses are actually frequently targeted by cyberattacks precisely because they're less likely to have robust security systems in place. According to industry data, a significant percentage of ransomware attacks hit businesses with fewer than 100 employees.

The good news is that cyber insurance has become more accessible and affordable for small businesses. Whether you need it depends on what data you handle and how your business operates. But if you're storing customer payment information, employee records, or proprietary client data in any form, it's worth discussing whether your current coverage leaves a gap.

QHow much does business insurance cost?

Honestly, the most accurate answer is: it depends, and here's exactly what it depends on.

Industry and type of work. A florist and a general contractor both need business insurance, but their risk profiles are very different. Industries with more physical risk, higher liability exposure, or professional responsibility typically pay more.

Revenue and business size. Premiums for general liability, in particular, are often tied to revenue or payroll because those are proxies for exposure. A one-person operation typically pays less than a business with twenty employees and multiple locations.

Coverage types and limits. More coverage costs more. Higher limits cost more. Whether you need a basic BOP or a full commercial package with professional liability, commercial auto, and cyber coverage makes a significant difference.

Claims history. If your business has had prior claims, some carriers will rate that higher risk into your premium.

QWhat is a deductible and how does it work?

Your deductible is the amount you pay out of pocket before your insurance kicks in on a claim. It's one of the most important numbers on your policy, and it's worth understanding clearly.

Here's a simple example. Say you have a $1,000 deductible on your homeowner's policy and a hailstorm damages your roof. The cost to repair the roof comes out to $6,000. Your insurance company pays $5,000. You pay the first $1,000. That's your deductible.

Deductibles exist because they reduce the number of small, frequent claims and help keep overall premiums more manageable for everyone. In exchange for paying a higher deductible, your monthly or annual premium is typically lower. A lower deductible usually means a higher premium.

On your auto policy, you'll typically have separate deductibles for collision coverage and comprehensive coverage. On your home policy, you might have a standard deductible for most claims and a separate, often larger, deductible specifically for windstorm and hail. In Texas, a lot of carriers have moved to percentage-based deductibles for wind and hail. That means instead of a flat dollar amount, your deductible is calculated as a percentage of your home's insured value. On a $300,000 home with a 2% wind and hail deductible, that's $6,000 out of pocket before coverage applies to storm damage.

Knowing your actual deductible amounts is step one in understanding what your policy will do for you in a real situation. If you're not sure what yours are, your declarations page, the summary at the front of your policy, will show them. And your agent can always walk you through it.

QWhat does "full coverage" really mean on auto insurance?

Here's a term that causes a lot of confusion. There's no official definition of "full coverage." It's a shorthand term people use, but different people mean different things when they say it.

What most people mean when they say full coverage is that they have liability coverage plus collision and comprehensive on top of it. Let's break those down.

Liability coverage is what Texas law requires you to carry. Under state law, you must have at least $30,000 per person and $60,000 per accident for bodily injury, plus $25,000 for property damage. That's the minimum. Liability covers the other party's damages when you cause an accident. It does not pay for your own vehicle or your own medical bills.

Collision coverage pays for damage to your own car in an accident, regardless of who's at fault.

Comprehensive coverage pays for damage to your car from things other than a collision: fire, theft, hail, flood, a deer running into the road, vandalism. Here in West Texas, where hail is a real consideration, comprehensive coverage is worth understanding.

So when someone says they have "full coverage," they usually mean they have all three. But full coverage doesn't mean unlimited coverage. Your policy still has limits, deductibles, and exclusions. It doesn't cover everything in every situation.

For example, even with what most people call full coverage, you may still owe money after a total loss if the payout doesn't match what you owe on your loan. That's where gap insurance comes in, and we'll cover that in a few minutes.

The practical point: rather than asking "do I have full coverage," the better question is what specific coverages do I have, what are the limits, and what are the deductibles. That's what actually matters when you have a claim.

QDoes home insurance cover flooding?

This is one of the most important things homeowners in Texas need to understand. The answer is no. Standard home insurance policies do not cover damage from flooding.

Flood is consistently listed as an excluded peril on standard homeowner's policies. The Texas Department of Insurance confirms this explicitly. Flood damage requires a separate flood insurance policy.

Flood insurance is available through the federal National Flood Insurance Program, the NFIP, and increasingly through some private carriers as well. Your home insurance agent can help you access flood coverage through either route.

Now here's something that surprises a lot of people. You don't have to be in a designated flood zone to flood. The Texas Department of Insurance notes that more than half of the homes flooded during Hurricane Harvey were outside of federally designated flood zones. Flooding can result from heavy rainfall in areas with poor drainage, from nearby creeks and drainage channels overflowing, or from situations that have nothing to do with a river or coastal storm.

If your lender requires flood insurance because your home is in a designated flood zone, you know you need it. But even if you're not in a required zone, a flood can still happen, and when it does, your standard homeowner's policy won't help.

One more thing to know: most flood policies through the NFIP have a 30-day waiting period before they go into effect. So you can't typically wait until a storm is approaching to buy coverage. Planning ahead matters here.

If you're not sure whether you should have flood coverage, that's a great conversation to have with your agent. It depends on where your home is, what the terrain looks like around you, and what you'd be able to absorb financially if the ground-floor level flooded.

QDo red cars cost more to insure?

No. The color of your car has no effect on your insurance rate. This is one of the most persistent myths in auto insurance.

Insurance companies don't ask about your car's color when underwriting a policy. They don't have a category for it in their rating systems. The color of the exterior is simply not a factor.

What does affect your auto insurance rate? According to the Texas Department of Insurance, carriers consider your driving record and claims history, where you park and keep the vehicle, the make and model of the car including its value and repair costs, how you use the vehicle such as whether it's for commuting or personal use, and in many cases your credit score.

The vehicle's make, model, and year matter because they affect how expensive it is to repair and how likely it is to be stolen. A luxury vehicle or a high-performance car will generally cost more to insure than a standard sedan, not because of its appearance but because of its repair costs and statistical loss data.

So if someone tells you that red cars cost more, you can politely correct them. The color doesn't matter. But the VIN, the driver's record, and the zip code do.

QDoes my credit score affect my car insurance in Texas?

Yes, it does. And this surprises a lot of people.

In Texas, insurance companies are permitted to use your credit history as a factor when setting your auto and homeowner's insurance rates. The Texas Department of Insurance confirms this, and it's a common practice across the industry.

Carriers don't pull your traditional credit score exactly. They use what's called an insurance score, which is built from your credit report but weighted differently. The factors they look at include how many open accounts you have, how much you owe relative to your available credit, whether you have past-due payments, and how frequently you've applied for new lines of credit.

The relationship between credit and insurance claims is statistical. Data over many years has shown that people with lower credit scores tend to file more claims. That's the actuarial basis for using it.

There are protections for consumers in Texas. Insurance companies cannot deny coverage or raise your rate solely based on your credit score. They cannot use medical debts that went to collections, nor credit checks related to insurance or mortgage applications, against you. And if your credit was negatively affected by things like a serious illness, the death of a spouse, a temporary job loss, a divorce, or identity theft, you have the right to ask the company to make an exception, and they must consider it.

The practical takeaway: improving your credit over time can benefit your insurance rates, not just your borrowing costs. And if your rate feels higher than you expected, it's worth asking your agent what factors are driving it.

QWhat is gap insurance and do I need it?

Gap insurance is a specific type of coverage that pays the difference between what you owe on your car loan and what your car is actually worth at the time of a total loss.

Here's how the gap happens. When you drive a new or newer vehicle off the lot, it begins depreciating immediately. Your car is now a used car, and its market value drops. Meanwhile, your loan balance doesn't drop as fast. If your car is totaled in an accident or stolen, your auto insurance will pay you what the car is currently worth, its actual cash value. But if you owe more on the loan than the car is worth, you're on the hook for the difference. That difference can be thousands of dollars.

Gap insurance, or guaranteed auto protection, covers that gap so you're not paying off a car you no longer have.

According to the Texas Department of Insurance, the gap is most likely to exist if your down payment was less than 20 percent, or if you financed for 60 months or more. Both situations mean you're building equity in the vehicle more slowly, which keeps you underwater on the loan longer.

You can typically purchase gap coverage through your auto insurance carrier, often at a lower cost than buying it through the dealership. If you're offered gap coverage at the dealership, compare prices. Your insurance agent may have a better option.

Once your loan balance drops below the vehicle's actual value, you no longer need gap insurance and can remove it to lower your premium. Check the math roughly every year or two. The TDI suggests canceling it once you owe less than the car is worth, which typically takes about two years.

QAm I covered if I drive for work or a rideshare company like Uber or Lyft?

This is an important one. The answer depends on exactly when and what you're doing.

For rideshare driving, there's a coverage gap that's well-documented. Your personal auto policy almost certainly excludes accidents that happen while you're driving for a transportation network company. The rideshare companies like Uber and Lyft do carry insurance, but the coverage varies significantly depending on which phase of the trip you're in.

Specifically: when you're offline, your personal auto policy applies normally. When you've turned the app on and are waiting for a ride request, you're in a gap. Uber and Lyft provide limited liability coverage during this phase, but it's often less than what you'd want. When you're actively on a trip with a passenger in the car, the rideshare company typically carries a $1 million liability policy.

The issue is that phase when you're available but haven't accepted a ride yet. If an accident happens then, you could find that neither your personal policy nor the rideshare company's full coverage applies.

The solution is a rideshare endorsement on your personal auto policy. Many carriers offer this now. It's usually not expensive and it fills that gap.

For driving for work in a personal vehicle, but not rideshare, the concern is similar. If you're regularly using your car to visit clients, make deliveries, or do other business activities, your personal policy may limit coverage for accidents that happen during those activities. Talk to your agent about whether your usage creates an exposure, and whether a commercial auto endorsement or a separate commercial auto policy makes sense.

The Texas Department of Insurance explicitly notes that most personal auto policies do not cover accidents that happen while driving for a ride-hailing service or delivering food or items for a fee.

QWhat is uninsured motorist coverage and do I need it in Texas?

Uninsured motorist coverage, often called UM coverage, pays for your medical bills and vehicle damage if you're hit by a driver who has no insurance, or by a hit-and-run driver.

Here's why it matters in Texas specifically. Texas law requires drivers to carry liability insurance, but a significant percentage of Texas drivers are uninsured at any given time. Various industry estimates place the rate of uninsured drivers in Texas somewhere between 14 and 20 percent. Out here in West Texas, the roads are long and traffic enforcement is spread thin. The chance of an encounter with an uninsured driver is real.

Under Texas law, insurance companies must offer uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage. You can decline it, but you have to do so in writing. The mere fact that carriers are required to offer it tells you something about how regulators view the risk.

Underinsured motorist coverage is related but slightly different. It applies when the other driver has insurance, but their limits aren't high enough to cover all your damages. Remember that Texas requires a minimum of $30,000 per person in liability. If you're seriously injured and your medical bills exceed that, underinsured motorist coverage on your own policy can make up the difference.

If you're in a hit-and-run accident, the Texas Department of Insurance notes that you must have reported it to the police for your UM coverage to apply. That's an important detail to know before you need it.

For a lot of drivers in Texas, uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage is one of the more straightforward additions to consider. It protects you when the other driver can't.

QHow much does adding a teen driver to my policy change things?

Honestly, adding a teen driver typically results in a meaningful premium increase. How much depends on a few factors, but it's rarely small, and it's worth understanding why.

Statistically, teen drivers are involved in accidents at significantly higher rates than experienced adult drivers. Insurance carriers price based on those statistics. A 16 or 17-year-old with a brand new license represents higher actuarial risk than a driver in their 30s or 40s with a clean record, and the rates reflect that.

What affects how much the increase will be: the teen's age and gender, the vehicle they'll primarily be driving, whether the teen was trained through a formal driver education program, and the insurance carrier's specific underwriting approach. A teen driving an older, lower-value vehicle often costs less to insure than a teen assigned to a newer or higher-value car.

Most carriers allow and generally prefer adding teen drivers to the family policy rather than writing a separate policy for them. The Texas Department of Insurance notes that adding teens to an existing policy is usually cheaper than a standalone policy.

Discounts can help. Many carriers offer a good student discount for teens who maintain a certain GPA, usually a B average or higher. Driver education completion may also qualify for a discount. Ask your agent specifically what discounts apply.

One practical note: the carrier may ask you to specifically list all licensed drivers in your household. Not disclosing a teen driver and then having them involved in an accident can create coverage complications. When your child gets their license, let your agent know.

QDoes bundling my home and auto insurance actually save me money?

Generally, yes. Bundling your home and auto policies with the same carrier usually results in a multi-policy discount on both. How significant that discount is varies by carrier, but 5 to 15 percent on each policy is a reasonable range to see.

But there's more to bundling than just the discount.

When your policies are with the same carrier, managing them is simpler. One contact, one renewal cycle to track, one billing relationship. If you have a claim that involves both your home and your car, such as a hail event that damages both your house and your vehicle, having both with the same carrier can simplify the claim process.

The discount doesn't always make bundling the right answer for every person. Sometimes the best rate for your auto coverage is with one company and the best rate for your home coverage is with another, and even after the bundle discount, keeping them separate comes out ahead. This is something to actually shop and compare.

Also, bundling works best when you're genuinely getting competitive rates from the same carrier. A multi-policy discount on an otherwise overpriced policy isn't the same as a good deal.

The right approach is to have your agent look at both scenarios, what you'd pay bundled versus separate, and let the numbers speak. If bundling saves money and simplifies your life, great. If not, it's still worth knowing.

QShould I drop collision coverage on an older car?

This is a question worth thinking through carefully because there's a point at which collision coverage stops making financial sense.

Here's the logic. Collision coverage pays to repair or replace your vehicle after an accident, but only up to the car's actual cash value. If your car is worth $4,000 and you have a $1,000 deductible, the most you could ever receive from a collision claim is $3,000. If your annual premium for collision coverage is $600, you're paying $600 per year to protect a maximum payout of $3,000.

There's no universal rule for exactly when to drop it, but a commonly used guideline is to consider dropping collision when the annual collision premium plus your deductible equals or exceeds the car's actual cash value. At that point, you're not gaining much from the coverage relative to what you're paying for it.

Actual cash value is not the same as what you paid for the car, or what you could get from a private sale. It's what the car is currently worth in the market, taking depreciation into account. Your agent or an online valuation tool can help you estimate it.

One counterpoint: if you couldn't replace the vehicle out of pocket if it were totaled, even a modest payout from a collision claim might matter more than the math suggests. That's a personal financial calculation.

And remember: dropping collision doesn't mean dropping comprehensive. Comprehensive covers things like hail, theft, fire, and animal collisions, and since it's separate from collision, you can keep it while dropping collision. For older vehicles in hail country, comprehensive alone often makes sense.
